# Results Summary

## Main Research Question
How does private-school access vary across Texas counties, and how is it related to income, poverty, and adult education level?

## Key Results

### Correlation Results
- Correlation between median income and private-school access across all Texas counties: 0.039
- Correlation between median income and private-school access among counties with population 50,000+: 0.284
- Correlation between poverty rate and private-school access among counties with population 50,000+: -0.151
- Correlation between bachelor's degree or higher percentage and private-school access among counties with population 50,000+: 0.446

## Interpretation
Across all Texas counties, median household income had almost no correlation with private-school access. Among counties with populations above 50,000, income showed a weak positive relationship with private-school access.

Poverty rate showed a weak negative relationship, meaning counties with higher poverty rates tended to have slightly fewer private schools per 100,000 people.

The strongest relationship was with adult education level. Counties with higher percentages of adults holding bachelor's degrees or higher tended to have greater private-school access.

## Regression Result
The regression model found that bachelor's degree attainment was the only statistically significant predictor among the variables tested. Median income, poverty rate, and population were not statistically significant in the model.

## Main Finding
Private-school access in larger Texas counties appears to be more strongly associated with adult education level than with median income or poverty rate alone.

## Limitations
This project shows association, not causation. County-level analysis can hide important differences within counties, and private-school access may also depend on other factors such as urbanization, religion, local history, and family preferences.
